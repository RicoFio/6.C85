Proposition: Northern America is A Main Contributor of Climate Change

In Favor of the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • In the left plot, which represents the per-capita GHG emissions in terms of CO2 equivalent metric tons, we set Asia as a delimiter of what can be considered "good" emissions through color-coding (targeting a Western audience). This is purposeful and gives the reader the impression that any emission level above this developing BRIC countries' are bad and are main contributors to this factor of climate change. A true delimitation does not exist, since the global GHG emissions are significantly higher than what would be naturally emitted through biochemical processes. The strategically placed legend gives a sense of just how high the global per-capita GHG emissions are while contemporarily warping the space between Europe, Northern America, and the worldwide emissions. Finally, we note that in an unbiased visualization, we would have used the global mean instead of the cumulative per-capita emissions (blue dotted line). However, in this case, the cumulative global emissions give a nice upper-bound which is far away from other values, giving us some less convoluted visuals. Finally, note the alarmingly red-colored area, which is meant to trigger a more affective reaction in the viewer to gain a qualifying view on the contributors.
  • Surface temperatures per capita is not a widely considered factor. Due to land properties and albedo, continents can have widely differing surface temperatures. However, the average change in temperatures represents the deviation from historical values, and while considering them in terms of per-capita is not a standard measure, it can inform us how anthropogenic changes to land and vegetation, such as parking, urbanization, industry, and deforestation, are impacting heat distributions. Similar to the plot below, I have removed comparability by excluding other continents. Coloring then indicates how temperature increases in Northern America are much more alarming than those seen on average. The choice of coloring the area below the dotted green line is purposeful and again indicates the viewer a "healthy" baseline, which, however, is arbitrarily set by me.
  • In conjunction, these visualizations picture a negative image of Northern America, providing factual blame on the continent's contribution to global climate change through both GHG emissions and increases in average surface temperatures. Thus, using these visualizations, the audience can easily take a stand in favor of the proposition, i.e., argue that Northern America is indeed one of the main contributors to climate change.

Opposed to the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • The design elements used in these two plots are similar but inverted to the above in order to counter the proposition. Instead of using per-capita normalization, I made use of absolute values. As one of the smaller continents, Northern America has a smaller impact on the global GHG emission balance. However, it actually contributes equally to the global average surface temperature increase which would be evident if the other continents would be displayed, and if we would remove the smoothing applied on the values of Northern America as explained below. .
  • In the left plot, we see the global CO2 equivalence of GHG emissions in metric tons, indicating that Asia is the main contributor to worldwide GHG emissions. We introduce green and red colored area, which, similar to the above, should give the audience a sense of good and "admissible" emissions. However, instead of using the line described by Northern America directly as a delimiter, we add a random offset to the boundary layer between the two areas to further elude the sensation that the emissions of both Europe and Northern America are better than a certain threshold.
  • In the right plot, the surface temperature increase for Northern America has been smoothed with a 10-year window rolling average to reduce variance and strengthen the illusion that Northern America's average surface temperature change has remained below the average world's level. Here, I have purposefully redacted other continents to limit comparability. Furthermore, through coloring, the visualization signals to the reader that Northern America represents the global minimum, with the rest of the world overshooting the green, i.e. "good" area.
  • Concludingly, both visualizations picture a deceptive picture, allowing viewers to oppose the proposition and believe that Northern America is not a main contributor to climate change.

Final Reflection

For the visualizations I have used the Quarterly Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Air Emissions Accounts (2010-2023, published March 05, 2024) and Annual Surface Temperature Change (1961-2022, published April 03, 2023) datasets published on the IMF Climate Change Dashboard. Furthermore, I have used the World Bank's Population (1961-2022, published 2022) dataset to accomplish the per-capita transformations. The aggregation of these datasets is not straight forward due to different naming and indexing conventions between the datasets. Moreover, some datasets list indicators by country and others per continent. Thus, I have aggregated all per-country datasets by continent and global numbers before creating the above visualizations.

I kept my visualizations simple and limited myself to a combination of line and area charts to enshrine the trust and credibility the reader can build towards the visualizations. The high data-ink ratio and soberness of the visualizations display a thorough understanding, a factual approach to the data, and allow for a natural causal reasoning with which the reader can argue for or against the proposition. I used smoothing and the per-capita "trick" to build my opposing arguments. On a global level, Northern America has a relatively small (but notable) influence on factors such as GHG emissions and their contribution to surface heat (think parking, sealed surfaces, urban dwellings, etc.). However, using per-capita considerations and a non-comparative analysis of the surface temperatures, we can make the case that Northern America's inhabitants are a major contributor to the climate change we are experiencing.

This exercise has truly shown me that visualizations, particularly the intent behind them, can make or break an argument. Through obfuscation, styling, and omission, it is possible to build a case with clear intent and persuade the reader to change their opinions.
